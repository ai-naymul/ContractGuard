# ContractGuard Knowledge Base
## Contract Clause Analysis Examples

This document contains examples of contract clause analysis for consumer protection.

---


## Example 1

### Contract Clause:
Tenant agrees that Landlord may terminate this lease at any time with 7 days written notice for any reason or no reason at all. Tenant waives all rights to contest such termination.

### Analysis:
## Analysis Results

**Clause Type:** Early Termination

**Risk Level:** 9/10

**Plain English Explanation:**
This clause allows your landlord to kick you out with only 7 days notice, for any reason. You're also giving up your right to fight it in court. This is extremely one-sided - most jurisdictions require 30-60 days notice and a valid reason.

**Red Flags:**
  • Only 7 days notice (most laws require 30-60 days)
  • Termination 'for any reason' - removes tenant protections
  • Waiver of legal rights to contest
  • Likely unenforceable but creates intimidation

**Suggestions:**
  • Request 30-day minimum notice period
  • Remove 'for any reason' language
  • Never waive your right to contest in court
  • Check local tenant protection laws - this may be illegal

### Contract Clause:
Security deposit shall be non-refundable. Landlord reserves the right to retain deposit for any damages, cleaning, or other expenses at Landlord's sole discretion without itemization.

### Analysis:
## Analysis Results

**Clause Type:** Security Deposit

**Risk Level:** 8/10

**Plain English Explanation:**
Your security deposit is being called 'non-refundable' which is illegal in most places. The landlord can keep your money without telling you why. This violates security deposit laws in most states.

**Red Flags:**
  • 'Non-refundable' security deposits are illegal in most jurisdictions
  • 'Sole discretion' means no accountability
  • No itemization requirement violates tenant laws
  • Vague 'other expenses' can mean anything

**Suggestions:**
  • Remove 'non-refundable' language immediately
  • Require itemized deduction list within legal timeframe
  • Add move-in inspection checklist
  • Research your state's security deposit laws

## Example 3

### Contract Clause:
Tenant shall be responsible for all repairs and maintenance including but not limited to plumbing, electrical, HVAC systems, and structural repairs regardless of cause.

### Analysis:
## Analysis Results

**Clause Type:** Maintenance Responsibility

**Risk Level:** 7/10

**Plain English Explanation:**
This makes you responsible for ALL repairs - even major ones like fixing the roof or electrical system. Normally landlords must maintain habitable conditions. This could cost you thousands.

**Red Flags:**
  • Structural repairs are typically landlord responsibility
  • HVAC and electrical systems are major expenses
  • 'Regardless of cause' includes normal wear and tear
  • May violate habitability requirements

**Suggestions:**
  • Landlord should be responsible for major systems (HVAC, electrical, plumbing)
  • Define 'normal wear and tear' exceptions
  • Set a dollar threshold (e.g., tenant pays repairs under $100)
  • Require landlord to maintain habitable conditions

## Example 6

### Contract Clause:
Borrower shall pay a prepayment penalty equal to 6 months of interest charges if loan is paid in full before the maturity date. This penalty applies to any refinancing, sale of property, or voluntary prepayment.

### Analysis:
## Analysis Results

**Clause Type:** Prepayment Penalty

**Risk Level:** 8/10

**Plain English Explanation:**
If you pay off this loan early - even by selling your house or refinancing for a better rate - you owe 6 months of extra interest. On a large loan, this could be thousands of dollars. This traps you in the loan.

## Example 7

### Contract Clause:
Interest rate shall be variable and adjusted monthly based on Lender's Prime Rate plus 8%. Lender reserves the right to modify the base rate calculation method at any time without notice to Borrower.

### Analysis:
## Analysis Results

**Clause Type:** Interest Rate

**Risk Level:** 9/10

**Plain English Explanation:**
Your interest rate can change every month AND the lender can change how they calculate it whenever they want without telling you. Prime + 8% is already high, but they can make it worse anytime.

**Red Flags:**
  • Monthly adjustments create payment uncertainty
  • 'Lender's Prime Rate' may differ from market prime rate
  • 8% margin is extremely high

## Example 8

### Contract Clause:
Any payment not received within 10 days of due date shall incur a late fee of $50 plus 2% of the payment amount. Additionally, interest rate shall increase by 3% for the remainder of the loan term.

### Analysis:
## Analysis Results

**Clause Type:** Late Payment

**Risk Level:** 7/10

**Plain English Explanation:**
One late payment permanently increases your interest rate by 3% for the entire loan. On a $200,000 loan, that's an extra $6,000+ per year. The late fee itself is reasonable, but the rate increase is punishing.

**Red Flags:**
  • Permanent interest rate increase for one late payment
  • 3% increase is severe and cumulative
  • No cure period to restore original rate
  • Combined penalties may exceed legal limits

**Suggestions:**
  • Remove permanent interest rate increase
  • Request cure period to restore rate after catching up
  • Cap total late fees per incident
  • Negotiate rate increase that expires after on-time payments

## Example 28

### Contract Clause:
Security deposit of $2,500 is non-refundable and shall be retained by Landlord upon termination of tenancy regardless of condition of premises. Landlord shall not be required to provide itemized statement of deductions.

### Analysis:
## Analysis Results

**Clause Type:** Security Deposit

**Risk Level:** 10/10 ⚠️ HIGH RISK

**Plain English Explanation:**
This clause tries to make your $2,500 security deposit completely non-refundable - meaning you'll never get it back no matter how perfectly you maintain the apartment. The landlord also claims they don't need to tell you why they're keeping your money. This is illegal in virtually every US state.

**Red Flags:**
• "Non-refundable security deposit" is ILLEGAL in most states - security deposits must be refundable by law
• No itemization requirement violates security deposit laws in all 50 states
• "Regardless of condition" means they keep it even if you leave the place spotless
• $2,500 may exceed legal limits in some jurisdictions
• This is a classic predatory landlord tactic

**Legal Context:**
Security deposit laws vary by state but ALL require: (1) deposits be refundable for legitimate damages only, (2) itemized deduction statements within 14-30 days, (3) return of unused portion. California limits deposits to 2 months rent; many states cap at 1-2 months.

**Suggestions:**
• DO NOT sign this clause - it is illegal and unenforceable
• Strike this language and replace with: "Security deposit refundable within [state requirement] days, less documented damages with itemized statement"
• Document the unit's condition with photos/video at move-in AND move-out
• Know your state's security deposit laws before signing anything
• Report this landlord to your local tenant rights organization
• This clause alone is grounds to walk away from this rental

## Example 29

### Contract Clause:
Landlord and Landlord's agents, employees, and contractors shall have the right to enter the premises at any time, without prior notice, for any purpose including but not limited to inspections, repairs, showing to prospective tenants or buyers, or any other reason.

### Analysis:
## Analysis Results

**Clause Type:** Right of Entry / Privacy

**Risk Level:** 8/10 ⚠️ HIGH RISK

**Plain English Explanation:**
This clause gives your landlord (and anyone they choose) the right to enter your home whenever they want, without telling you first, for any reason at all. They could walk in while you're sleeping, showering, or not home. This violates your reasonable expectation of privacy and likely breaks state law.

**Red Flags:**
• "At any time" includes middle of the night, weekends, holidays
• "Without prior notice" violates tenant privacy laws in virtually every state
• "Any purpose" is dangerously vague and unlimited
• Includes "agents, employees, and contractors" - unlimited people can enter
• "Prospective tenants or buyers" implies they may be selling - are you being displaced?
• No emergency exception language - suggests they just want unlimited access

**Legal Context:**
Most states require 24-48 hours written notice before non-emergency entry. Entry must be at "reasonable hours" (typically 8am-6pm). Landlords cannot enter for harassment or without legitimate purpose. Violation of these rules can be grounds for lease termination by tenant.

**Suggestions:**
• Require 24-48 hours written notice for ALL non-emergency entries
• Limit entry to reasonable hours (9am-6pm on weekdays, 10am-4pm weekends)
• Define specific permitted purposes (repairs, inspections, emergencies)
• Require your consent for showing to prospective tenants
• Add: "Landlord shall knock and announce before entering"
• Document any unauthorized entries as potential harassment

## Example 30

### Contract Clause:
Tenant shall be responsible for all repairs and maintenance of the premises including but not limited to plumbing, electrical systems, HVAC, appliances, structural repairs, roof, foundation, and all other components regardless of cause of damage or normal wear and tear.

### Analysis:
## Analysis Results

**Clause Type:** Maintenance Responsibility

**Risk Level:** 9/10 ⚠️ HIGH RISK

**Plain English Explanation:**
This clause makes YOU responsible for fixing everything in the property - including major systems like the roof, foundation, plumbing, and electrical. If the 20-year-old furnace dies in winter? You pay. Roof leaks? Your problem. This could cost you tens of thousands of dollars and likely violates habitability laws.

**Red Flags:**
• Structural repairs (roof, foundation) are ALWAYS landlord responsibility
• HVAC, plumbing, electrical are major systems - typically landlord's duty
• "Regardless of cause" includes pre-existing problems and normal wear
• "Normal wear and tear" exemption is legally required in most states
• This effectively makes you responsible for a property you don't own
• Violates implied warranty of habitability

**Legal Context:**
The implied warranty of habitability (recognized in 49 states) requires landlords to maintain: structural integrity, plumbing, heating, electrical, and sanitation. Tenants typically responsible only for: minor repairs under $50-100, damage they cause, and keeping unit clean. Landlords cannot contract away habitability obligations in most states.

**Suggestions:**
• Remove all major systems from tenant responsibility (HVAC, plumbing, electrical, structural, roof, foundation, appliances provided by landlord)
• Add explicit "normal wear and tear" exception
• Limit tenant repairs to damage directly caused by tenant and minor repairs under $100
• Add: "Landlord shall maintain premises in habitable condition as required by law"
• Request home warranty or maintenance plan from landlord
• This clause is likely unenforceable - but indicates a slumlord mentality
